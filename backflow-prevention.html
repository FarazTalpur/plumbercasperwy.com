Backflow occurs when water flows backwards through your plumbing system, potentially carrying harmful substances from contaminated sources into your clean water supply. Frequently Asked Questions

Backflow prevention is the process of stopping contaminated water from flowing back into clean water supplies. It typically involves the use of devices such as backflow preventers to ensure that harmful pollutants do not compromise the safety of drinking water.

Backflow prevention is crucial for protecting public health by ensuring that drinking water remains uncontaminated. Without proper measures, contaminants from sources like irrigation systems or industrial processes could flow back into the main water supply.

A backflow preventer is a device that allows water to flow in only one direction, preventing reverse flow. It typically uses a valve with springs or gates that close if the water attempts to move in the opposite direction.

Backflow prevention devices should be tested annually by a certified professional to ensure they are functioning properly and providing adequate protection against contaminant intrusion into the water supply.

Common types of backflow prevention devices include air gap, double check valve assembly (DCVA), reduced pressure zone (RPZ) assembly, and pressure vacuum breaker (PVB). Each type serves different applications and provides varying levels of protection.
